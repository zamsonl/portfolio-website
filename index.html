Running in production Flagship

Enterprise SOC Build-out

In August 2025 my employer was hit by ransomware. There was no SOC, no central logging, and no way to reconstruct what had happened. I was brought in to make sure it could not happen the same way twice — and over the following months designed and deployed a full Security Operations Centre from nothing.

  • SIEM at the centre A Wazuh all-in-one deployment (manager, indexer, dashboard) on Ubuntu, tuned at the systemd level to stay stable under sustained ingest.
  • Six log sources, one pipeline Firewall, core switching, wireless access points, Windows endpoints, Active Directory, and a Zeek network sensor — normalised through custom ingest pipelines that resolve field-mapping conflicts between sources.
  • Detection content written, not downloaded Custom multi-level decoders and correlation rules built for the estate’s own hardware, rather than relying on generic rulesets that never fire.
  • Alerting that reaches a human A real-time bot pushes critical alerts to chat, so triage does not depend on somebody happening to be looking at the dashboard.
  • Hardened access path The dashboard gateway was migrated from an ad-hoc Python proxy to Nginx with TLS, subnet-level allow-listing, and host firewall hardening.

04 — Background

About

I am a Year 3 Cybersecurity student at Asia Pacific University who also runs security operations for a licensed gaming operator — and I learn best by building things that have to survive contact with real traffic.

The turning point was a ransomware incident in August 2025. There was no central logging, so nobody could answer the first question anyone asks after an incident: what actually happened, and when. Everything I have built since comes back to that — getting the telemetry in, making it queryable, and turning it into something an analyst can act on before the next incident, not after.

A lot of that work is unglamorous debugging: tracing why a dashboard tile reads “—” back through the proxy, the index pattern, and the query window until the pipeline is actually correct rather than merely quiet. That habit of chasing a silent failure to its root is the part I would bring to a blue team.

I work both sides. The vulnerability assessment I ran on our own network taught me more about what to detect than any ruleset would have. Right now I am extending the SOC from detection into automated containment, and looking for a team where I can learn from people who have done this at a larger scale.
